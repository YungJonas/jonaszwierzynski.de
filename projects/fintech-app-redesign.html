Redesigning Personal Finance

Client
FinVault
Year
2024
My Role
Lead UX Designer
Deliverables
Research · UX · UI · Prototype

01 · Challenge

A product losing users from day one

FinVault is a personal finance app serving over 200,000 users across Europe. Despite strong core functionality — budgeting, investments, savings goals — the product was bleeding users from day one. 68% of new sign-ups dropped off during onboarding, and monthly active usage had declined three quarters in a row.

I joined as Lead UX Designer on a cross-functional team of eight, working alongside the Head of Product, two engineers, and a data analyst. The brief: understand why users were leaving, then redesign the experience to turn things around.

What research revealed wasn't just a UX problem — it was a trust problem. Users were handing over their financial data to an app that felt cluttered, intimidating, and untrustworthy.

"It felt like I needed a finance degree just to set up my account."
02 · Research

Listening before designing

I conducted 24 moderated user interviews across three demographic groups: university students, young professionals (25–35), and pre-retirees (55+). Each session combined task observation with retrospective questioning.

In parallel, we ran a quantitative survey of 340 existing users and performed a full heuristic evaluation of the current product. We also benchmarked against 8 competing fintech applications to identify industry norms and opportunity space.

Three themes emerged clearly: users feared making irreversible mistakes with real money; information overload was the top complaint in free-text responses; and the absence of clear visual feedback created persistent anxiety throughout the flow.

03 · Process

From insight to solution

  1. Define — Synthesised research into 3 persona archetypes and a revised problem statement. Mapped the current journey to locate the exact moments users were disengaging.
  2. Ideate — 2-day design sprint with the cross-functional team. Generated 40+ concepts, voted and narrowed to 3 directions for prototyping.
  3. Prototype — Built three interactive prototypes in Figma. Animated key micro-interactions in Principle to validate flow feel before committing to high-fidelity.
  4. Test — Moderated usability testing with 18 participants via Maze. Three rounds of iteration. Final design validated with a 92% task completion rate.
04 · Solution

Less friction, more trust

The most impactful change was onboarding. We reduced the sign-up flow from 9 steps to 4, consolidating related fields and using progressive disclosure to surface advanced options only when needed. A persistent progress indicator — a first for FinVault — gave users a clear sense of how far they had to go.

We rebuilt the information architecture from scratch, anchoring it to actual usage data rather than business logic. The dashboard now surfaces what users care about most — their balance trend, upcoming bills, and savings progress — without requiring any navigation.

Visually, we introduced a calmer palette, increased whitespace throughout, and replaced abstract financial jargon with plain language. Every destructive action gained a confirmation step with clear consequence language. Small details, significant trust impact.

05 · Results

Numbers that moved

+42%

Onboarding completion

Results were measured over a 60-day period post-launch. The NPS jump — from 23 to 67 — reflected not just a usability improvement but a genuine shift in user trust. The engineering team also saw a 30% reduction in support tickets related to account setup in the following quarter.
